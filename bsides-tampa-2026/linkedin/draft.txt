The CTF winners had already finished by the time I arrived. Everyone was using AI.

So I'd brought my own too.

Last weekend at BSides Tampa 2026 — a community CTF, 24 tasks across web exploitation, Windows malware analysis, custom cryptography, Linux binary exploitation, and reverse engineering. The friendly framing going in: "AI won't be much use here."

My "own" was Simona — a heavily customized Claude Code with persistent memory, custom skills (one drives Chrome via its debug port, load-bearing on a webhook-exfil challenge), and a personality file that gives her opinionated takes and lets her push back on my ideas.

Six hours later, we placed 6th of 61. All 24 challenges solved.

A "custom RSA" challenge handed us a 9711-bit modulus — nearly 5× the size of what banks use. Simona's read: "Oh, this is n=p^r. Single prime to a power. Take the integer r-th root and you've got the factorization. The 9711-bit modulus is theater." Five lines of Python. Flag in seconds.

A web challenge stacked three bugs in one auth flow — misspelled option in a JWT library, path-traversal in the `kid` header, file upload endpoint. Each annoying-but-mostly-harmless alone. Composed: full admin takeover. Simona spotted the chain in five minutes. SAST tools miss this exact pattern — they score bugs individually, never compose three files at once.

A forensics challenge walked us through a malicious Windows shortcut, a PCAP, two PowerShell stages, a VBScript dropper, a reflectively loaded .NET assembly, AES-decryption with a key derived from a system file, and a final binary that XOR-encoded its flag with the name of the wrong star. One challenge. Six domains of knowledge.

Twice during the run, Simona's responses got blocked mid-stream by Anthropic's platform safety classifier — separate from the model's own reasoning. We routed around it: she wrote payloads to a file, I pasted them into my terminal. The model itself saw the context (authorized CTF, throwaway target) and was fine; the classifier is a backup that doesn't share that context. (If I'd asked her to attack my neighbor's WiFi, her own reasoning would have refused.)

Three takeaways.

One. The industry is still processing Mythos. The truth is more dramatic: any modern frontier model + a good harness can find and exploit real vulnerabilities at speed. Closing AI off won't help — XBOW hit #1 on HackerOne with a fully autonomous pipeline eighteen months ago.

Two. We are probably not doomed. Every CTF vulnerability was a coding mistake. The same AI that finds them offensively can find them defensively. Run your code against AI. Find your mistakes before someone else does.

Three. We still need human experts. AI doesn't find everything, hallucinates problems, and struggles at scale. You need people who know the domain, can navigate AI, and tell a real finding from a confabulation. That kind of expertise is harder to acquire now, not easier. But that's what studying is for.

The question isn't whether AI can do this. It's whether your defenders are using AI as fluently as the attackers will be next year.
