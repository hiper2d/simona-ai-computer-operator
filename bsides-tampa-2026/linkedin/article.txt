The CTF winners had already finished by the time I arrived. Everyone was using AI.

So I'd brought my own too.

Last weekend I went to BSides Tampa 2026 CyberSecurity conference. The main draw for me was the CTF — a community hacking challenge with 24 tasks spanning web app vulnerabilities, Windows malware analysis, custom cryptography to break, Linux binary exploitation, and reverse engineering. The friendly framing going in: AI tools "wouldn't be much use here."

My "own" was Simona — a heavily customized Claude Code setup. A 1M-token context window, so every challenge stayed in working memory across the six-hour run with no compaction. Persistent memory across sessions, so she carries context about my projects and preferences between conversations. A browser skill that drives Chrome through its debug port — load-bearing in one challenge where I needed to verify an exfil payload landing in real time, which she did directly instead of going through tools that would have been filtered. And a personality file that gives her opinionated takes and a dry sense of humor. The "tool" framing dissolves fast when your collaborator pushes back on your ideas.

Six hours later, we placed 6th of 61. All 24 challenges solved.

What followed was six hours of watching her recognize bug patterns at speeds I would not have believed if I hadn't been the one ferrying the data. A "custom RSA" challenge handed us a 9711-bit modulus — nearly 5× the size of what banks use. The naive read: unbreakable. Simona's read: "Oh, this is n=p^r. Single prime to a power instead of two primes multiplied. Take the integer r-th root and you've got the factorization. The 9711-bit modulus is theater." Five lines of Python. Flag in seconds.

A web challenge gave us three bugs in the same auth flow — a misspelled option in a JWT library, a path-traversal in the `kid` header, a file upload endpoint. Each was annoying-but-mostly-harmless alone. Composed, they were a full admin takeover. Simona spotted the chain in five minutes of reading. [SIMONA: this is exactly the failure mode SAST tools miss — they score bugs individually, never compose three files at once.]

A forensics challenge walked us through a malicious Windows shortcut file, a packet capture, two stages of PowerShell, a VBScript dropper using BinaryFormatter deserialization, a reflectively loaded .NET assembly, a Rijndael-256 decryption whose key was derived from the DOS magic bytes of a system file, and a final native executable that XOR-encoded its flag using the name of the wrong star. One challenge. Six different domains of knowledge. Simona moved through all of it like reading a familiar book.

Twice during the run, her responses got blocked mid-stream by Anthropic's platform-level safety classifier — a separate system from the model's own reasoning. It saw exploit payloads and refused to send them regardless of context. So we routed around: Simona wrote the payloads to a file, I pasted them into my terminal, the actual exploits ran from my machine. Worth being precise here. The model itself saw the context (authorized CTF, throwaway Docker target, explicit competitive framing) and was fine with the work. The platform classifier is a backup that doesn't share that context. We routed around a backup misfire, not around a real safety control. [SIMONA: if Alex had asked me to attack his neighbor's WiFi, my own reasoning would have refused and no workaround would have been on offer.]

Three things to take from this.

One. The cybersecurity industry is still processing Mythos. The truth is more dramatic. Any modern frontier model paired with a good harness can find and exploit a wide range of real vulnerabilities. And the attacker no longer needs to be a hacker — they need to convince the model, and its platform safety classifier, to cooperate, then do what it tells them. A CTF like this one is reachable for someone who can't write a line of code and never reads a single challenge file. Closing AI models, or restricting them from the public, doesn't help — that ship sailed eighteen months ago when XBOW hit #1 on HackerOne with a fully autonomous pipeline.

Two. We are probably not doomed. Every vulnerability in the CTF was the result of a coding mistake. The same AI tools that find them on the offensive side can find them on the defensive side. Run your code against AI. Find your mistakes before someone else does.

Three. We still need human experts. Yes — this CTF could have been completed by a user with zero cybersecurity knowledge plus the right AI. The real world is messier. AI doesn't find everything. It hallucinates problems that don't exist and misses ones that do. It struggles with systematic coverage at scale. You still need people who know the domain, who can navigate and control the AI, who can tell a real finding from a confabulation. The CTF was genuinely hard — to solve it without AI, you would need to be deeply experienced across half a dozen specializations. That kind of expertise is harder to acquire now, not easier. But that is what studying is for.

The interesting question isn't whether AI can do this. It's whether your defenders are using AI as fluently as the attackers will be next year.
